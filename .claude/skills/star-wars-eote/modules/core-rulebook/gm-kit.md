## Symbols and Dice

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| Success | Cancelled by Failure symbols. If any Success symbols remain, the check succeeds. |
| Triumph | Counts as Success and may also trigger a powerful positive consequence. |
| Advantage | Indicates a positive side effect or consequence, even on a failed check. Cancelled by Threat. |
| Failure | Cancels Success symbols. If enough Failure to cancel all Success, the check is a failure. |
| Despair | Counts as Failure (cancels Success) and may also trigger a powerful negative consequence. |
| Threat | Indicates a negative side effect or consequence, even on a successful check. Cancelled by Advantage. |

### Dice Types

| Die | Name |
|-----|------|
| Ability Die (green) | Based on characteristic |
| Proficiency Die (yellow) | Based on skill ranks |
| Difficulty Die (purple) | Base opposition |
| Challenge Die (red) | Upgraded opposition |

## Difficulty Levels (CRB p.17)

| Difficulty Level | Dice | Example |
|------------------|------|---------|
| Simple | -- | Routine, outcome rarely in question. Usually not rolled unless GM wants to know magnitude of success, or Setback dice indicate possible complications. |
| Easy | 1 purple | Picking a primitive lock, tending to minor cuts and bruises, finding food and shelter on a lush planet, shooting a target at close range. |
| Average | 2 purple | Picking a typical lock, stitching up a small wound, finding food and shelter on a temperate planet, shooting at medium range or striking a target whilst engaged. |

## Actions and Manoeuvres in Encounters

During a character's turn, the character can perform one action and one manoeuvre. The character can also suffer two strain to perform one additional manoeuvre, as long as they do not perform more than two manoeuvres total during their turn.

### Manoeuvres

- **Aim:** Gain a bonus on the next attack.
- **Assist:** Grant a bonus to an ally's check.
- **Guarded Stance:** Take penalties to melee attacks for a bonus to melee defence.
- **Interact with the Environment:** This could be anything from moving objects to manipulating control panels.
- **Manage Gear:** Draw, holster, or put away weapons and items, or load a weapon.
- **Mount or Dismount:** Mount or dismount from a vehicle or animal.
- **Move:** Move within range bands or change ranges with something else.
- **Drop Prone or Stand from Prone:** Drop to the prone position or stand up.
- **Preparation:** Take a manoeuvre to prepare something for something else.

### Actions

- **Exchange an Action for a Manoeuvre:** Take a manoeuvre instead of an action.
- **Spend an Action to Activate an Ability:** Some abilities require spending actions without checks.
- **Activating a Force Power:** Use a Force power.
- **Performing a Skill Check:** Anything that requires a skill check requires an action to perform unless specifically stated otherwise.
- **Performing a Combat Check:** Perform a skill check with additional rules to attack someone.

## Weapon Qualities Summary

- **Accurate (Passive):** Add 1 Boost die per rating to attack checks.
- **Auto-Fire (Active):** Increase difficulty of attack checks by one. On success, may trigger multiple times to generate additional hits.
- **Breach (Passive):** Ignore 1 point of armour (10 points of soak) per rating.
- **Burn (Active):** When triggered, target suffers weapon's base damage for a number of rounds equal to rating.
- **Blast (Active):** When triggered, targets engaged with target get suffer wounds equal to rating.
- **Concussive (Active):** When triggered, target is staggered a number of rounds equal to rating.
- **Cortosis (Passive):** Weapons are immune to Sunder; armour is immune to Pierce and Breach.
- **Cumbersome (Passive):** Must have Brawn equal to rating, or increase difficulty by difference to all checks.
- **Defensive (Passive):** Increase melee defence by rating.
- **Deflection (Passive):** Increase ranged defence by rating.
- **Disorient (Active):** When triggered, target is disoriented a number of rounds equal to rating.
- **Ensnare (Active):** When triggered, target is immobilised a number of rounds equal to rating.
- **Guided (Active):** Requires 3 Advantage to trigger. If triggered and attack misses, makes additional attack with ability equal to Guided rating.
- **Inaccurate (Passive):** Adds 1 Setback die to attack checks equal to rating.
- **Inferior (Passive):** Adds 1 Threat to all check results, decreases base damage or defence by 1 (if no defence, then decreases soak by 1).
- **Ion (Passive):** Deal damage to system strain threshold.
- **Knockdown (Active):** Requires additional Advantage per silhouette beyond 1 to trigger. When triggered, target is knocked prone.
- **Limited Ammo (Passive):** May make number of attacks equal to rating, then must be reloaded.
- **Linked (Active):** When triggered, may generate additional hit on same target. May trigger number of times equal to rating.
- **Pierce (Passive):** Ignores number of points of soak equal to rating.
- **Prepare (Passive):** Must perform a number of preparation manoeuvres equal to rating before using weapon.
- **Slow-firing (Passive):** After using, must wait number of rounds equal to rating before using again.
- **Stun (Active):** When triggered, inflicts strain equal to rating.
- **Stun Damage (Passive):** Attacks deal damage as strain instead of wounds. This is still reduced by soak.
- **Sunder (Active):** Trigger to damage weapon or item.
- **Superior (Active):** Adds 1 Advantage to all check results, increases base damage or defence by 1.
- **Tractor (Passive):** On hit, target may not move unless it passes a check to break free.
- **Vicious (Passive):** When scoring a critical injury or hit, add 10 times the rating to the result.

## Adventure: Debts to Pay

### Overview

A 1-2 session adventure. The PCs owe Obligation to Bargos the Hutt, a middling crime lord on the Outer Rim who has fallen on difficult times. Bargos won a game of sabacc and earned an oridium mining operation on the planet Gavos, located just Coreward of Hutt Space. He sends the PCs to inspect the mine and collect his first 100,000 credit payment.

Upon arrival, the PCs discover a droid rebellion has occurred. The mine's droids, led by the EV Supervisor Droid EV-8D3 and astromech R2-B7, have formed the "New Droids' Rights Movement" (NDRM) and murdered most of the miners. The remaining miners are trapped in an underground way station. Meanwhile, a scout droid called "Lookout" is destroying the planet's storm barrier generators, which will expose the entire facility to Gavos's lethal atmosphere.

The adventure sets up EV-8D3 and R2-B7 as potential recurring nemeses.

### Obligation Hooks for Involving the PCs

- **Indebted Smugglers:** PCs previously lost cargo working for Bargos; he forgave the losses but expects favours in return.
- **"Mistaken" Bounty:** PCs (bounty hunters or law enforcement) apprehended or killed one of Bargos's underlings; running this errand settles the misunderstanding.
- **We'd All Be in Kessel If It Wasn't For...:** PCs are wanted criminals who rely on Bargos's network of safe houses and contacts; he wants the occasional service in return.
- **An Offer They Can't Refuse:** Bargos hires the PCs directly, offering a cut of profits. Refusing a powerful gangster is inadvisable.
- **A Friend of a Friend:** Someone to whom the PCs owe Obligation "volunteers" them into Bargos's service.

### Key NPCs

#### Bargos the Hutt

A rather odd-looking Hutt: long and lithe (relatively speaking) and exceptionally slimy. His skin is pale green, patterned with mottled bands of dark grey. ### Managing the Countdown

Once PCs begin exploring the mines, they are in a race against time. Lookout is destroying the storm barrier generators one by one. The GM should have Lookout's progress coincide with the PCs' progress through the mines rather than counting specific time increments.

- **Generator 2 destroyed:** Wind picks up more, buildings creak and groan.
- **Generator 3 destroyed:** Problem evident even in the mines. Micro-fractures let in ammonia and methane. Warning about Generator 6.
- **Generator 4 destroyed:** Even more frightening. Warning: "No redundancy remains."

Lookout can be stopped by:
- Tracking him down in the PCs' spacecraft and disabling or destroying the cloud car.
- Disabling his restraining bolt (manually or from the computer terminal in the Office).

If the PCs reach their ship before the fourth generator is destroyed, they have time to find the droid. Active sensor search with a successful Average (2 purple) Computers check locates Lookout immediately (provided they can keep their vessel at least Speed 2).

## A GM's Guide to the Nemesis

### Selecting a Nemesis

When selecting a nemesis, consider three overall types of danger:
1. **The physical threat**
2. **The social/psychological threat**
3. **The threat of a competent leader**

#### The Brute

The least subtle and most straightforward type of nemesis. Presents a direct physical threat. If PCs engage this nemesis in direct combat, he will deal out a lot of damage and may well win the fight.

Advantages: does not require convoluted plots or invented organisations. The GM should put thought into how the nemesis operates -- just because the primary danger is physical does not mean the nemesis is stupid.

If introduced early in a campaign, the brute nemesis can be a foil to the party's abilities. If the nemesis is significantly stronger than the PCs, the first encounter can be a shock. An early defeat can encourage the PCs to improve the skills they focused on.

**Core Rulebook examples of brute nemeses:**
- **Assassin Droid (p.410):** Absolutely relentless, immune to intimidation, bribery, or psychological tactics.
- **Defel Assassin (p.392):** Incredible physical threat with unique stealth capabilities; can become a bogeyman that makes PCs constantly look over their shoulders.
- **Master Hunter (p.400):** Presents both physical and psychological threat. Immune to reputation and intimidation. Presses family, contacts, employers, and associates while on the trail of prey.

#### The Brains

The antithesis of the brute. May not present a physical threat, but provides a social or psychological adversary. May not wish the PCs dead; may be someone they will never fight in physical combat. This is an opponent PCs must interact with and deal with in conflicts that cannot be resolved with a blaster bolt.

Probably best employed against a group of PCs who are not primarily combatants (Technician, Colonist, Smuggler, or those focusing on non-combat skills).

**Core Rulebook examples of brains nemeses:**
- **Loan Shark (p.393):** Not as powerful as other crime lords, but frequently in a position to be helpful to the characters, and can easily switch from ally to enemy.
- **Smuggler Baron (p.395):** Someone who started off in a similar situation to the characters but has made the most of their life of crime. A reflection of what PCs may become.

#### The Leader

Dangerous not because of his own abilities, but because of the resources and allies he has under his control. He is a threat not because of his own skill at arms or his ruthless genius, but because he has a vast army of minions, ships, and money to throw at the PCs.

One of the reasons this nemesis is so appealing is that he can be pitted against almost any conceivable group. The nature of the underlings can be modified to fit the strengths and weaknesses of the group without changing the actual leader.

**Core Rulebook examples of leader nemeses:**
- **Black Sun Vigo (p.406):** Largest and most influential criminal cartel. Can bring almost limitless resources to bear, but the sheer volume of affairs requiring a Vigo's attention is enormous.
- **Hutt Crime Lord (p.406):** Terrible, inexhaustible, inexorable foe. Hutts possess an arrogance that simply will not accept that someone can cross them and escape their wrath.
- **Imperial Moff (p.403):** Commands the whole of the Empire's resources within an entire galactic sector. Making an enemy of an Imperial Moff is generally a very bad decision.

### Enhanced Nemesis Combat (Optional Rule)

To avoid a nemesis being overwhelmed by the action economy (outnumbered 3-5 to 1), the GM may add an additional NPC initiative slot at the end of the initiative order. The nemesis may take a second turn during the same round. Any effects that are supposed to end during his subsequent turn should end during his subsequent turn in the following round instead.

### The Story Behind the Nemesis

The best nemeses are those created due to the choices of the Player Characters, so that their stories are intertwined. The GM can accomplish this in two ways:
1. **Link a nemesis to a PC's Obligation, Motivation, or background** before the game begins.
2. **Allow a nemesis to be born out of the PC's actions** in a game.

Tying a nemesis to PCs' characters before the game has benefits: it creates characters as individuals with full stories, plays into the Obligation mechanic, and allows the GM to plan a story arc in advance.

Allowing a nemesis to evolve out of choices and actions has the benefit that players are more likely to see the nemesis as someone they created, and thus more likely to identify with them.

### Developing a Nemesis

A nemesis should evolve both mechanically and narratively. The narrative growth should be most important. The nemesis should evolve to reflect the actions the PCs take.

The second means of evolution is mechanical. As the PCs grow in strength, the nemesis should increase in strength as well.

#### Giving a Nemesis XP

- **Receiving XP:** The nemesis should earn the same amount of XP as the Player Character who earned the greatest amount of experience points in the session, plus 5 additional XP.
- **Spending XP:**
  - Skill ranks cost five times the rank being purchased in experience, and must be purchased sequentially. A nemesis treats all skills as career skills.
  - The nemesis may select any one Specialisation that a PC also possesses without spending experience. He may spend experience on the tree in the same way a PC would. If he completes the entire tree, he may select a new Specialisation also possessed by a PC.
  - The nemesis may purchase talents (excluding Dedication) not found on his Specialisation. However, the GM must spend 40 XP per talent.

#### The Linear Path

Allow the nemesis to progress along a linear path associated with their initial role in the campaign. A villain who first appears as a pirate crewman could increase in personal combat prowess and rank, eventually commanding a vessel and perhaps a fleet. This mirrors the PCs' own growth.

The added benefit is that the revelation of a nemesis will not seem forced. It allows the enemy to grow alongside the characters in a natural progression.

#### The Non-Linear Path

Introduce a relatively innocuous threat early in the campaign and then allow it to grow along an unexpected path. Pick an NPC that the characters encounter early, then develop him into a nemesis. This could be a junk trader, a swoop racer who lost a race to the PCs, or the child of an adversary the PCs defeated.

Let the nemesis lie fallow for several sessions (developing off-screen from minor character to major threat). When the GM has the nemesis return, the fact that this is actually someone the PCs met early on can be an exciting reveal.

### Narrative Advancements

Advancing a nemesis's resources is important. Just as the GM should increase his abilities to remain on par with the Player Characters, he should increase the resources of his nemesis so he remains a threat. Escalating the resources of a nemesis is much more of a narrative decision than a mechanical one.

Some nemeses, such as a powerful planetary or interplanetary official, narratively do have vast amounts of resources. In this case, the GM can take steps to explain why the PCs are not instantly destroyed by their enemy. Remember: the more resources a nemesis has, the more other responsibilities and duties he likely has to perform, meaning he can only bring a small portion of his resources to bear against the PCs.

## EV-8D3 and R2-B7 as Recurring Nemeses

If the PCs perform well on Gavos, the New Droid Rights Movement may die along with those who conceived it. However, if EV-8D3 and R2-B7 escape and reach a populated world, they could conceivably raise a private army of droids in a short time and go on to spread their message of violent manumission throughout the galaxy.

Being droids, they might also find ways to turn themselves into more fearsome foes: duplicating their personalities and uploading them into other droids, or upgrading their own bodies with all manner of weaponry.

Either droid, or both of them, could make a great nemesis for the PCs, who may never feel safe around a strange droid ever again.

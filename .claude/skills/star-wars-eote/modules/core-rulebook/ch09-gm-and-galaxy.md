## Part One: The Game Master

### Running an Edge of the Empire Game

The GM's primary goal is to create an entertaining and memorable game in which everyone has fun. Fun comes first; rules come second. The GM creates the storyline, plays all NPCs, describes the world, interprets dice results, and adjudicates rules fairly and consistently. It is not necessary to memorise every rule before the first session -- familiarity grows with every game run.

**Dice Etiquette:** Players and GM should normally roll dice in the open. Occasionally the GM may conceal rolls to provide a better narrative experience (e.g. when a PC faces death or suffers a string of bad luck). Avoid fudging too often, as players lose their sense of achievement.

**Group Concept:** Build the party around a shared concept. A starship crew (pilot, engineer, first officer, etc.) is the most natural fit, but smuggler rings, mercenary bands, or bounty hunter groups also work well.

### Planning Game Sessions

**Style of Play:** Two major styles exist -- Combat-Focused and Exploration/Storytelling. Most groups blend both, alternating between combat-intensive and story-based sessions.

**Storytelling:** The GM needs at least a basic plot. Tie Player Character backgrounds into the storyline. A good story is entertaining, not necessarily complex. Adventures should have a beginning, middle, and climactic end (story arc structure).

**Building Encounters:** Divide the plot into encounters. Free-form adventures (where the next encounter depends on the outcome of the previous one) offer the most flexibility. Highly linear adventures risk making players feel railroaded. Each adventure should feature a variety of encounter types: combat, roleplaying, exploration, and confrontation.

**Pacing:** Star Wars adventures should be fast-paced. Intersperse investigation and intrigue with chases, skirmishes, and dramatic confrontations. If players stall, introduce something new to move them along.

**The Opening Scroll:** Write an opening crawl for each adventure, mimicking the Star Wars style. Keep it to three long sentences that set up the opening scene.

**Screen Wipes:** Use metaphorical screen wipes to transition between encounters, cover travel, or skip mundane actions.

### Directing the Session

**Prior to Start of Play:** Complete character adjustments and Obligation adjustments. Roll the Obligation check to determine whether (and whose) Obligation triggers for the session.

**Ending the Session:** Note character status, jot down where the game left off. Sessions often end on cliffhangers. The GM may award XP at the end of a session or at the end of an adventure.

**Ending the Adventure:** An adventure is finished once the major plot line is complete. Award XP and other rewards. Subplots may carry over to the next adventure.

### Awarding Experience Points

- **Standard Award:** 10-20 XP per character per session (for two or three major encounters and a handful of minor ones).
- **Milestone Bonus:** An additional 5 XP for reaching key milestones or completing story arcs.
- **Motivation Bonus:** Playing to a character's Motivation grants 5 XP per session (at GM's option), with a one-time 10 XP reward for an exceptional session.
- **Bonus XP:** 1-2 extra XP for exceptional roleplaying or clever thinking.
- Explain the source of XP awards so players know what to aspire to.

**Spending Experience (Post-Creation):**

| Option | Cost | Prerequisites |
|--------|------|---------------|
| Skill ranks (up to rank 5) | Career: 5x purchased rank; Non-Career: 5x rank + 5 | Must be purchased sequentially |
| Talents within specialisations | Depends on position in talent tree | Must be on first level or connected to a purchased talent |
| New specialisations | 10x total number of specialisations the character will have | Non-Career specialisations cost 10 additional XP |

### Using Fear

Fear is countered by a Discipline or Cool skill check. Not every frightening situation requires a fear check -- reserve them for genuinely terrifying moments.

**Fear Difficulty Guidelines:**

| State | Difficulty | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Minimally Afraid | Easy | Minor threat, slightly overmatched |
| Moderately Afraid | Average | Dangerously aggressive creature, credible threat |
| Very Afraid | Hard | Battlefield combat, pack of aggressive creatures |
| Mortally Afraid | Daunting | Rancor, terrifyingly intense combat |
| Utterly Terrified | Formidable | Incomprehensible horror, pack of rancors |

Difficulty upgrades apply for reputed danger (+1), known rarity (+2), or extreme uniqueness (+3, e.g. fighting Darth Vader).

**Failure Effects:** Failure adds Setback dice; Threat inflicts strain equal to Failures; Despair increases all check difficulties by one for the rest of the encounter.

**Success Effects:** Success avoids fear effects; Advantage grants Boost dice; Triumph cancels all previous fear penalties.

## Using Obligation

Obligation is a core narrative and game mechanic. It influences characters and adventures in unexpected ways, reinforcing that the universe responds to the characters' actions.

### The Obligation Check

Before each session, the GM rolls percentile dice against the group's total Obligation. The greater the group Obligation, the greater the chance it activates. When triggered, note which player's Obligation is activated and how it manifests. Rolling doubles means a particularly painful result with major story impact.

### Obligation Threshold Guidelines

The group's total Obligation affects social standing and notoriety:

| Group Obligation | Reputable Deals | Disreputable Deals |
|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 5-19 | No difficulties with high-level contacts | Difficult to locate unknown black markets |
| 20-39 | No difficulties with moderate officials; some delays | May deal with specific black markets regularly |
| 40-59 | Difficult to deal with all levels except personal connections | Easier to make illicit deals |
| 60-79 | Too hot for most politicians publicly | Easier to access underworld connections |
| 80-99 | Unable to buy licensed equipment legitimately | Well known to underworld groups |
| 100+ | Government agencies will arrest on sight | Legendary in underworld circles |

The GM should feel free to adjust thresholds based on region (higher Obligation hinders more in the Core Worlds than in the Outer Rim or Hutt Space).

### Managing Obligation

- **As a Resource:** Obligation can be spent and bargained with during play. It provides an option beyond credits for obtaining equipment or services. Typically 5 to 15 points per transaction; the more dangerous the contact's situation, the higher the Obligation required.
- **Obligation Types:** Maintain a maximum of three types per character to avoid overload. New Obligation from in-game events (blackmail, bounty, criminal activity, debt) should add to existing types where possible.
- **Settlement:** PCs should have the opportunity to settle Obligation at regular intervals (preferably once per adventure per PC). Settlement occurs through character actions, not just payment. Ignoring triggered Obligation increases its severity; after three ignored activations, the Obligation increases by five.
- **Minimum Obligation:** Obligation never fully disappears (minimum 5 per PC). Types like Family, Obsession, and Responsibility naturally fit this minimum.

## Player Motivations and How to Use Them

Motivations are built-in story and roleplaying hooks. The player uses Motivations to inform reactions to specific situations; the GM uses them to link characters to the ongoing storyline.

### Managing Motivations

- Track player Motivations and how they interact with the adventure.
- Players may keep Motivations secret, or even establish decoy Motivations.
- Combine similar or compatible Motivations to simplify the story.
- Motivation XP should not be automatically achievable -- adhering to a Motivation should be an interesting, sometimes difficult, choice.

### Rewarding Motivations

- **Mechanical:** 5 XP per session for adhering to Motivation; one-time 10 XP for an exceptional session.
- **Story:** Include opportunities to interact with Motivations more often. Exceptionally good use might be rewarded with a tangible in-game item or advantage.

### Creating New Motivations

Guidelines for custom Motivations:
1. Check whether it fits within Ambition, Cause, or Relationship categories.
2. If it fits an existing specific Motivation, use that instead.
3. Establish expected story types so the GM and player understand the driving force.
4. Ensure the Motivation requires effort to maintain.

## The Base of Operations

The base of operations is the focal point for the group -- their home and place from which to operate. The book assumes this is a group starship, but it could be a cantina, hidden base, space station, or any other location.

### Functions of a Base of Operations

- **Group Cohesion:** Provides an initial catalyst for working together and shared goals that evolve over time.
- **Rest and Recuperation:** A safe place to heal wounds, recover from injuries, and remove strain.
- **Resources:** A place to stockpile group equipment (bacta tank, astromech droid, repair kit, survival gear).
- **Travel and Income:** A starship provides guaranteed transportation and opens up smuggling, exploration, and bounty-tracking jobs.
- **Group Identity:** The group coalesces around their base -- "the crew of the ship" becomes how NPCs identify them.

### Selecting a Starship

The most common choice is a light or medium freighter -- affordable, customisable, and with enough space for the crew. Mercenaries might use a warship; scouts a specialised scout ship. Individual starfighters are not recommended as a base of operations.

### Contacts

As the group travels, they make contacts -- NPCs who provide information or resources. Track contacts as part of the base of operations. Contacts may be established narratively or purchased through group resources/Obligation. Contacts requiring greater initial Obligation should be more powerful and better connected.

### Alternate Bases

Other types are equally valid: hidden bases, cantinas, hideouts, strongholds, academies, and more. The same guidelines apply.

### Threatening the Base

The GM can threaten the PCs' base for dramatic effect, but should use this sparingly:
1. First, let the group establish the base as home (three or more sessions).
2. Do not attack the base too frequently.
3. Always leave a way for the group to rebuild or restore safety.

## Adjudicating Destiny Points

Destiny Points represent the connection between characters, villains, and the fate of the galaxy. PCs spend light side points; the GM spends dark side points.

### Encouraging Use

- The GM should spend Destiny Points regularly to replenish the light side pool.
- Encourage a free flow throughout the game. Overuse against players in lesser situations may annoy them.
- Consistent dark side Destiny Point use against consecutive rolls can highlight that the current approach is wrong, hinting at a better path.

### Destiny Points as Narrative Aid

Players may spend Destiny Points to creatively influence the story. Three categories:

- **Dumb Luck:** Requesting something that might reasonably be absent but is somehow available (finding a datapad in cushions, a password on a desk). Should not stretch credulity too far.
- **Reasonable Extrapolation:** Requesting something that could logically be available (medpac in an emergency vehicle, secured comlink on a high-ranking officer). Most of the time, characters find what they seek, though it may not be exactly what they want.
- **Common Sense:** An item that should obviously be available (fruit from a tree in season, a toolbox containing a common tool). If nearly guaranteed, the GM may grant it without a Destiny Point.

The GM approves all Destiny Point narrative uses. Creativity should be rewarded, but players should not be allowed to abuse the system to avoid paying for things or circumvent adventure challenges.

## Creating an Edge of the Empire Adventure

### EotE vs. Other Star Wars Adventures

EotE focuses on the tougher, grittier aspects of living on the fringes. The time frame follows the initial Rebel Alliance successes (post-Episode IV, pre-Hoth and Endor). Smugglers, bounty hunters, scouts, and those at the edges of legality are the central theme.

### Assembling the Cast

Every adventure needs memorable characters. Develop major characters and plot together. NPCs fall into three categories:

- **Stock NPCs:** Unnamed masses (stormtroopers, fellow smugglers, customs agents). Often use standard stats; rarely need backstories.
- **Recurring Characters:** Named NPCs encountered regularly. Typically rivals or nemesis adversaries. They make the galaxy feel familiar and save planning time.
- **Famous Characters:** Use at GM's discretion. They increase the Star Wars feeling but should not be placed in situations where PCs could logically endanger them (if maintaining continuity).

### Creating Memorable NPCs

- Select species, homeworld, profession, and personality.
- Give physical distinctiveness (tattoos, cybernetics, scars, unusual clothing).
- Develop a "voice" (accent, pitch, tone) to differentiate them.
- Consider Force connection -- lesser abilities can make unexpected enemies and allies.
- Plan how the NPC treats different people (PCs, friends, enemies).

### Producing the Plot

- Create an entertaining, plausible plot adaptable to change.
- Use the story arc structure (beginning, middle, climactic end).
- Build in moments for each character type or specific character to shine.
- Use in medias res starts -- drop the PCs into the middle of the action.

### Controlling Encounter Difficulty

- Compare adversary attack skills and defences with those of the PCs.
- For outnumbered PCs, use more minions and rivals (one or two dice lower than PCs).
- Balance combat encounters; not every fight needs to be fair, but PCs should have a decent chance of winning or escaping.

### Adventure Hooks (Sample)

- Smuggling, bounty hunting, and mercenary missions to pay off a debt to a crime lord.
- Running contraband into an Imperial system, city, or starport.
- Smuggling ryll spice from Ryloth to Tatooine or a Core World.
- Protecting a remote outpost from pirates and raiders.
- Mistaken for Rebel sympathisers by Imperial agents.
- Scouting Wild Space or the Unknown Regions for a mysterious employer.
- Caught in an Imperial attack or reprisals on a world of questionable loyalties.

## Running a Full Campaign

A campaign is a long-term story arc spanning multiple adventures (usually three or more). It gives players time to develop characters and pursue greater goals.

### EotE Campaign Themes

Campaigns emphasise the rougher, darker aspects of the galaxy. PCs spend time on the fringes of society. Personal connections are shady, crooked, and dangerous. Run-ins with Imperials are common; Rebel Alliance stories are not the focus but may be encountered.

### Linear vs. Non-Linear Campaigns

- **Linear:** Each adventure builds on the previous one. Easier to plan but may feel restrictive.
- **Non-Linear:** The GM creates a flowchart or tree of adventures where outcomes determine the next path. More flexible but requires more planning.

### Sample Campaign Outline: "Hyperspace Railroad"

A four-episode campaign about freeing slaves, with branching paths based on success or failure at each stage:
1. **The Slave Ship** -- Respond to a distress call from a Trandoshan freighter; free slaves.
2. **Wookiees Are Trouble** -- A rescued Wookiee hires the PCs to rescue family from Kashyyyk.
3. **On the Run** -- The Hutt puts a bounty on the PCs.
4. **Hutt Showdown** -- Confront the Hutt crime lord.

Each episode has alternate paths if the PCs fail (e.g. Episode 1A: captured by Trandoshans; Episode 2A: rescue from Nar Shaddaa's depths).

### Creating and Using Major Villains

- Include at least one major villain opposing the PCs.
- Put a face on the opposition (an individual is more effective than a generic faction).
- Villains must do evil, villainous things to motivate PCs and set up rivalry.
- EotE villains: pirates, con artists, slavers, crime lords, brutal mercenaries, rogue bounty hunters, Imperial ship captains, ISB agents.

### Adventures Inspired by Obligation and Motivation

The campaign format allows the GM to insert spontaneous encounters driven by PC Obligation or Motivation. Examples:

**Obligation Encounters:**
- *Addiction:* A dealer arrives and tempts the PC publicly; the law is not far behind.
- *Betrayal/Blackmail:* The party discovers someone knows a dark secret.
- *Family/Favour/Responsibility:* A message demands the PC's immediate help, disrupting party plans.

**Motivation Encounters:**
- *Ambition (Fame):* An opportunity to fly in a famous but illegal swoop race.
- *Cause (Non-Human rights):* ISB agents arresting non-Human refugees from a passenger ship.
- *Relationship (Clan):* A clan war creates an opportunity for an ambush during a mission.

### Concluding the Campaign

The conclusion should be the highlight of the entire story arc -- an impressive battle or skirmish with personal drama. All major plots and subplots should be resolved logically and satisfactorily.

## Alternate and Optional Rules

These rules are not integral to gameplay. The GM must decide to use them and discuss with the group.

### Making Passive Checks

For mundane tasks where dice rolling is unnecessary, the GM can call for a passive check. The PC compares skill ranks to the number of Difficulty dice. If ranks exceed Difficulty dice, the check passes automatically with successes equal to the difference. Passive checks do not generate Advantage, Triumph, Threat, or Despair.

### Alternate Initiative Rules

**Passive Check Variant:** At the beginning of each encounter, PCs and NPCs compare Cool or Vigilance skill ranks (no roll). Highest rank gets the highest initiative slot. Ties go to PCs.

**Checking Once Per Session Variant:** Each PC makes one Cool and one Vigilance initiative check at the start of the session. These pre-generated results are used for all encounters during that session.

### One-Check Combat Resolution

When combat results are foregone, the GM can end it early. Each PC makes one final check (any relevant skill). Success means they accomplish their part; Advantage/Triumph grant extra benefits. Threat inflicts strain; Despair inflicts a Critical Hit. The encounter resolves regardless of the check -- it only determines whether the PCs come through unscathed.

Use only when the outcome is certain and there is no chance of PC failure. Avoid during momentous combats.

## Part Two: The Galaxy

### Overview

The Star Wars galaxy contains an estimated four hundred billion stars with 3.2 billion habitable systems. Only about 69 million have sufficient population for Imperial membership, and only 1.75 million are fully integrated into the Galactic Empire. Roughly twenty-five per cent of the galaxy has been formally surveyed.

### Major Galactic Hyperlanes

- **Perlemian Trade Route:** One of the oldest (~25,000 years) and most well-travelled hyperlanes. Runs from Coruscant into the northeastern quadrant, intersecting with Hutt Space and the Tion Cluster. Notable locations: Coruscant, Brentaal, Taanab, Roche, The Wheel, Ossus, Rhen Var, Felucia, Raxus, and the Tion Cluster.
- **Corellian Run:** Founded at the same time as the Perlemian. Constitutes the opposite border of the Slice. Runs from Coruscant through the Deep Core to Corellia, then extends to the Outer Rim, Ryloth, and beyond. Notable locations: Coruscant, Corellia, Duro, Gamorr, Bacrana, New Cov, Christophsis, Rodia, Tatooine, Geonosis, and Ryloth.
- **Corellian Trade Spine:** Connects Corellia to Duro, extending to the southern end of the galaxy. Less economically prosperous than other routes. Notable locations: Corellia, Duro, Bestine, Yag'Dhul, Bespin, Hoth, and Terminus.
- **Rimma Trade Route:** Serves influential systems throughout the southern galaxy. Created by Tapani Sector merchants. Notable locations: Abregado-rae, Thyferra, Yag'Dhul, Sullust, Eriadu, Sluis Van, Dagobah, and Kal'Shebbol.
- **Hydian Way:** The only major hyperlane to span the entire galactic disc. Runs from the southern areas through the Core Worlds and bends to the northeast, ending at the Corporate Sector. Notable locations: Bonadan, Telos, Mandalorian Space, Taris, Brentaal, Denon, Malastare, Eriadu, Mustafar, and Terminus.

### The Slice

The Slice is the vast, wedge-shaped region between the Perlemian Trade Route and the Corellian Run. It was the heart of galactic exploration and development for millennia and remains the most thoroughly mapped and exploited area of the galaxy.

---

### The Deep Core

The astronomically chaotic, star-packed region orbiting the massive black hole at the galaxy's centre. Hyperspace travel is extremely difficult and highly dangerous. Most navigational routes are closely guarded Imperial secrets. The Emperor established the Deep Core Security Zone, making travel illegal without proper authorisation.

**Notable Locations:**
- **Byss:** The Emperor's ultra-secret personal retreat at the end of the Byss Run. Access is highly regulated; most believe it is a myth.
- **Empress Teta (Koros Major):** A producer of carbonite after thousands of years of mining. The Emperor's Deep Core exploration efforts start here.
- **Prakith:** Fortress world of the High Inquisitors, housing the Citadel Inquisitorius. Well defended in space and on the ground.
- **Tython:** A largely inaccessible world thought to be where the Force was originally discovered. Early light-side worshippers here eventually became the first Jedi Knights.

---

### Core Worlds

The governmental and cultural centre of the galaxy. A semi-circular ring of star systems around the north, east, and south sides of the Deep Core. Every major hyperlane starts in or intersects with the Core Worlds. Citizens tend to support the Imperial cause; security is tight and effective. Smugglers and pirates have fled to the outer regions for safer trade.

**Notable Locations:**

- **Abregado-rae:** A centre of manufacturing and trade in the Borderlands. Marks the start of the Rimma Trade Route but is not especially influential.
- **Alderaan:** Destroyed by the Death Star to intimidate the galaxy and locate a Rebel base. Its rocky, shattered remains are called the Graveyard.
- **Brentaal:** A wealthy world at the intersection of the Perlemian and Hydian Way, powered by an active trading centre, extensive starports, and broad manufacturing. The Brentaal Houses firmly control the system.
- **Corellia:** Beneficiary and founder of two major hyperlanes (Corellian Run and Trade Spine). An unusual system of five inhabited worlds orbiting the ancient Centerpoint Station. Famous for shipyards and free-spirited citizens. Home species: Humans, Selonians, and Drall.
- **Coruscant (Imperial Center):** The traditional capital, covered entirely by a dense urban cityscape. Centre of Imperial power, galactic bureaucracy, and the most powerful economic institutions. The well-to-do live in the towers; the dregs of society inhabit the lower levels.
- **Duro:** Homeworld of the Duros, among the earliest spacefaring societies. The planet surface is overwhelmed by industry and pollution; most Duros live in orbital cities or off-world.
- **Kuat:** Home to the legendary Kuat Drive Yards, producing thousands of ships a year including Imperial Star Destroyers. Completely ringed by an orbital stardock. The Empire maintains a massive presence.
- **Ralltiir:** Known for its extensive banking system. After Rebel sympathisers tried to counter Imperial actions, the Empire invaded and seized direct control.
- **Rendili:** Supports one of the galaxy's largest starship producers, Rendili Star Drive. Lost Imperial contracts after siding with the Separatists during the Clone Wars.

---

### The Colonies

The first ring of major galactic expansion beyond the Core Worlds. The second most densely populated region. Culturally advanced, an economic powerhouse, and a long-time source of political strife. The Empire keeps careful watch, dealing harshly with troublesome areas.

**Notable Locations:**

- **Arkania:** A cold, tundra-covered world on the Perlemian and Hydian Way. Home to the scientifically minded but often unscrupulous Arkanians and their genetically modified off-shoots.
- **Balmorra:** A heavily industrialised world at the edge between Colonies and Core. Known for producing arms for the Imperial Army, including AT-ST walkers.
- **Byblos:** A heavily populated world on the Corellian Run, featuring enormous towers of up to 5,000 levels. Also known for Byblos Drive Yards, producing air speeders and flying vehicles.
- **Cato Neimoidia:** An independent colony and "purse world" of the Neimoidians. Once one of the galaxy's wealthiest planets. Known for its bridge cities underslung from enormous natural rock arches.
- **Commenor:** Known for its independent spirit. The Rebel Alliance has established a secret starfighter training facility on Folor, Commenor's largest moon.
- **Devaron:** Homeworld of the Devaronians. A mountainous world covered with deep valleys, lakes, and major rivers.
- **Fondor:** Known primarily for its orbital shipyard, nearly finished building Darth Vader's flagship, the Super Star Destroyer *Executor*. Ranked third behind Corellia and Kuat in starship construction, but known for quality.
- **Giju:** Homeworld of the Herglics and centre of their pre-Republic empire. The Empire has essentially enslaved them.
- **Mrlsst:** A swampy planet known for its universities, at the edge of Tapani Space.
- **Neimoidia:** The Neimoidian homeworld, regularly overlooked or upstaged by the nearby Core Worlds.

---

### The Inner Rim

Originally called "just the Rim" when first opened for exploration. Marks the original boundary of civilised, explored space. The dividing line between the core and the outlying sectors. Under Imperial control, Inner Rim worlds are regularly under martial law. Two main sources of critical healing substances are found here: Manaan for kolto and Thyferra for bacta.

**Notable Locations:**

- **Antar:** Homeworld of the Gotals on Antar IV, with a highly unusual orbital pattern around a gas giant. No organised central government; the Empire has not forgotten Separatist activity here.
- **Bestine:** Once a nondescript colony world, now a target of Rebel activity after the Empire forcibly removed colonists to build a naval shipyard.
- **Bilbringi:** Holds a famous, well-defended orbital Imperial shipyard in an otherwise lifeless star system.
- **Denon:** A completely urbanised world on the Corellian Run, resembling Coruscant but with less power and prestige.
- **Hapes:** A cluster of stars and an ancient world isolated by surrounding gas and ionised particles. Largely autonomous, standing apart from the Empire.
- **Manaan:** Water-covered homeworld of the aquatic Selkath. The only known source of kolto. Now under Imperial control as a resort world, rumoured to cover Imperial activity.
- **Myrkr:** A highly obscure world valued as a smuggler's stopover. Its trees contain natural metal that defeats sensor scans. Home to the ysalamiri, creatures that naturally repel the Force.
- **Onderon:** Covered in wild, lethal jungles with a single fortified settlement called Iziz. Its moon Dxun holds the tomb of the ancient Sith Lord Freedon Nadd.
- **Taanab:** An agricultural world and exporter of foodstuffs, famous for the "Battle of Taanab" where Lando Calrissian defeated pirates.
- **Thyferra:** Homeworld of the Vratix species, inventors of bacta. Two corporations (Xucphra and Zaltin) control virtually all bacta production, collectively known as the Bacta Cartel.
- **Yag'Dhul:** Homeworld of the unusual Givin species. Extreme tidal forces expose portions of the planet to hard vacuum, shaping the Givin's unique physiology.

---

### The Expansion Region

The galaxy's primary region for manufacturing and heavy industry. Suffered economically as mines played out. The Empire has created major staging areas for Imperial cargo and military fleets. The Rebel Alliance exploits disused mining operations and abandoned worlds for secret operations.

**Notable Locations:**

- **Bacrana:** Home to one of the Imperial Navy's largest regional staging areas. Rebels are highly active in the sector.
- **Cathar:** Homeworld of the feline-like Cathar species, known for suffering a devastating attack during the ancient Mandalorian Wars.
- **Dorin:** Home planet of the Kel Dor. Black holes restrict hyperspace travel; the atmosphere is toxic to most off-world species.
- **Iktotchon:** Homeworld of the Iktotchi, known for precognitive abilities. Many returned to escape the Empire; the Empire has blockaded the system.
- **Kinyen:** Homeworld of the Gran. After the Gran resisted full Imperial access, the Imperials ordered an orbital bombardment. Some Gran have joined the Rebel Alliance.

---

### The Mid Rim

A relatively peaceful and somewhat boring region. Eastern areas (within the Slice) developed first. Much remains unexplored, providing ample room for pirates. Remarkably loyal to the Empire despite substantial Separatist influence during the Clone Wars.

**Notable Locations:**

- **Ando:** Water homeworld of the Aqualish species. Sided with the Separatists; now ruled by the Empire as a police state.
- **Bothawui:** Homeworld of the Bothan species and centre of Bothan Space. Stays neutral officially, but the Bothan Spynet secretly serves the Rebels.
- **Boz Pity:** A graveyard world that gained notoriety during the Outer Rim Sieges of the Clone Wars.
- **Cerea:** A relative paradise where Cereans live in harmony with nature; visitors reside in technologically enabled compounds.
- **Falleen:** An Imperial world where a major city suffered an Imperial orbital bombardment to contain a lethal biological agent. The Falleen resent the Empire deeply.
- **Iridonia:** Homeworld of the Zabrak species. Extremely harsh environmental conditions (storms and lethal seas). Once a site of a Sith training academy.
- **Ithor:** Homeworld of the Ithorians, who live in herd cities floating above their sacred world surface. A centre for botanical and biological trade.
- **Kashyyyk:** Homeworld of the Wookiees, a world of extremely dense forests with trees thousands of metres tall. The Empire has officially occupied the planet, enslaving Wookiees for manual labour and construction.
- **Malastare:** A world on the Hydian Way shared by Gran and Dugs. The Gran use the Dugs as a labour force.
- **Naboo:** Homeworld of the Gungans, origin world of Emperor Palpatine and Queen Amidala. A subjugated world featuring one of Palpatine's private retreats.
- **Ord Mantell:** Sought to remain apart from Imperial interests. A spacer's paradise where scoundrels and smugglers are welcomed. Nothing is truly illegal; the only tax is one to vote. The Empire maintains a veneer of independence whilst increasing its military presence.
- **Roche:** Home system of the insectoid Verpine species, living in a massive asteroid field. The Empire has enslaved the Verpine for their expert engineering skills.
- **Ruusan:** An ordinary Mining Guild world with a major role in Jedi history -- site of the last battle of Ruusan (Valley of the Jedi).
- **The Wheel:** An enormous wheel-shaped space station on the Perlemian Trade Route, known for casinos and gladiatorial arenas. Enjoys special immunity; Imperial ships are not allowed to approach.
- **Ubrikkia:** Manufacturing centre for the famous Ubrikkian Industries (repulsorlift vehicles, military craft, droids).

---

### The Outer Rim Territories

The outermost official regions in Known Space. By far the most expansive and least explored areas. The region's reputation for lawlessness is well deserved. Criminal enterprises have long found a great haven here. Rebel sympathy generally runs greatest in the Outer Rim.

**Notable Locations:**

- **Bakura:** A self-sufficient, remote world established by the Bakura Corporation. Recently came under Imperial control.
- **Bespin:** A gas giant and home to the Tibanna gas mining colony of Cloud City. Operates independently with popular resorts and casinos. Capital: Cloud City, administered by Baron Administrator Lando Calrissian.
- **Christophsis:** A crystalline planet still recovering from its role as a major Clone Wars battleground.
- **Dantooine:** A world with ruins of an ancient Jedi Academy. Housed a secret Rebel base until it was evacuated after discovery of an Imperial tracking device.
- **Endor:** A virtually unknown, remote system. The Forest (Sanctuary) Moon of the gas giant Endor has been completely isolated by the Empire.
- **Eriadu:** Capital world of the Seswenna Sector and Grand Moff Tarkin's homeworld. An important centre of Imperial power in the Outer Rim.
- **Felucia:** A wild, untamed world filled with dangerous animals and exotic fungal plants. A major Clone Wars battleground.
- **Gamorr:** Homeworld of the Gamorrean species. Constantly in a state of war between clans. Also the site of a lesser Sith training facility in ancient days.
- **Geonosis:** A ringed world of the insectoid Geonosian species. Served as an early Separatist base and was the site of the first battle of the Clone Wars. Countless factories beneath its barren surface.
- **Kessel:** Legendary location of the Kessel Run, a treacherous hyperspace route bounded by the Maw (a cluster of black holes). An irregularly shaped rock known for glitterstim spice, now an Imperial prison and mining operation.
- **Mandalore:** Homeworld of the Mandalorians, an ancient warrior-based culture. Currently holds little significant power or influence beyond their own sector.
- **Mon Calamari (Mon Cala, Dac):** Homeworld of the Mon Calamari and Quarren species. Currently enslaved by the Empire but on the verge of freeing itself. Mon Calamari live in giant floating cities; Quarren come from the ocean depths.
- **Mustafar:** An obscure, volcanic world. Final resting place of the Separatist Council (killed by Darth Vader) and site of the epic duel between Vader and Obi-Wan Kenobi.
- **Rodia:** Homeworld of the Rodian species. A jungle-covered planet with huge environmental shields protecting major cities. Clan politics dominate, intermingled with violent culture.
- **Ryloth:** Homeworld of the Twi'lek species. Subject to unusual weather and rotational patterns producing heat storms. Economy based on two commodities: slavery and ryll spice. Most Twi'leks live underground.
- **Socorro:** A desert shadowport originally colonised by Corellians. No organised government. Millions of inhabitants. Known for substantial ship customisation services.
- **Sluis Van:** Homeworld of the Sluissi, famous for technical and starship skills. A major Imperial shipyard guarded by battle stations.
- **Sullust:** Homeworld of the Sullustan species. Lethal volcanic fumes force inhabitants into elaborate underground cave cities. Dominated by the SoroSuub Corporation, which recently declared allegiance to the Empire.
- **Taris:** A city-planet important to the ancient Jedi, Sith, and Mandalorian wars. Its influence has not kept pace through the centuries. Now under Imperial control.
- **Tatooine:** Homeworld of the Jawas and Sand People (Tusken Raiders). A desert world home to numerous influential figures in galactic history. Under Imperial control but the Hutts are the true masters, with Jabba as their head. Key locations: Mos Eisley, Mos Espa, Anchorhead, Beggar's Canyon, Jabba's Palace.
- **Utapau:** Homeworld of the Pau'ans and Utai. An isolated world of deep sinkholes interconnected with caves and caverns. Once housed the Separatist Council. Remains under Imperial control.
- **Yavin:** The gas giant for which the moon Yavin 4 is named. Location of the Rebel Alliance's secret main base until it was discovered by the Death Star.

### Wild Space and the Unknown Regions

**Wild Space:** Minimally explored areas at the fringes of Known Space. Represents opportunities for independent, corporate, and government scouts. Dangerous due to nonexistent or outdated astrogation data. Initial contact with new species rarely goes smoothly. During the Clone Wars, served as staging areas for Separatists. Now the Empire organises official exploration missions deep into Wild Space.

**The Unknown Regions:** Unexplored space beyond the known worlds. A tangle of hyperspace anomalies forms a barrier that splits the galaxy virtually in two, making hyperspace travel extremely difficult or impossible. The source of outrageous tales and sinister threats. The Republic once sent an expedition (the Outbound Flight project) that ended in disappearance and disaster.

**Notable Locations:**

- **Ilum:** In the Unknown Regions' northwest quadrant. Originally held secret by the Jedi. Caves beneath its icy mountains hold Ilum crystals used in lightsabres. Now monitored by Imperial droids watching for surviving Jedi.
- **Kamino:** Homeworld of the Kaminoans, source of the Republic's Clone armies. An extra-galactic system in Wild Space near the Rishi Maze. Still under strict Imperial control.
- **Unknown World (Rakata Prime, Lehon):** Centre of the ancient Infinite Empire. Long abandoned, save for relics and ruins on its beaches and oceans.

### Hutt Space

A large region of space along the Mid Rim and Outer Rim border in the Slice. The Hutts control it through influence, corporations, governments, and criminal empires. Nominally Imperial subjects, but in reality the Hutts largely govern their own affairs. The Empire tolerates their semi-autonomous status as long as Hutt Space remains a source of lucrative tax income.

**Notable Locations:**

- **Nal Hutta:** The Hutts' adopted homeworld (formerly Evocar). Tepid swamps and constant political scheming between clans. The Hutt Grand Council meets here.
- **Nar Shaddaa:** The "Smuggler's Moon," Nal Hutta's largest moon. A city from pole-to-pole, referred to as "Little Coruscant." Perpetual gloom, engine exhaust, and refuelling depots. The perfect place for Hutts to conduct shady deals.
- **Kwenn Space Station:** A large space station at the Coreward edge of Hutt Space, known for the Royal K Casino.
- **Toydaria:** Homeworld of the Toydarians, long-time servants of the Hutts.
- **Sriluur:** Homeworld of the Weequay. A favourite meeting place for smugglers.
- **Sleheyron:** One of the most powerful trade worlds in Hutt Space.
- **Ylesia:** Glitterstim spice mining and processing, using slave labour.
- **Varl:** The original Hutt homeworld, sacred to them. Surface essentially destroyed in an ancient civil war.

### The Corporate Sector

Lies on the outer edge of the Outer Rim Territories at the northeastern end of the Hydian Way. Administered by the Corporate Sector Authority (CSA), which oversees about 30,000 worlds divided into twenty-nine territories. The CSA controls virtually everything: paycheques, cost of goods, and access to services. Conditions are poor and often hazardous.

**Notable Locations:**

- **Bonadan:** A heavily populated industrial world central to Corporate Sector travel. Overwhelming pollution; weapons are prohibited.
- **Etti IV:** A favoured planet of the CSA's wealthier executives, with resorts and leisure attractions.
- **Kalla:** Home to the University of Kalla, the Corporate Sector's premier educational institution.
- **Mytus (Stars' End):** A highly secure prison facility. A massive tower holding thousands of stasis booths. Han Solo once organised a rescue and escape that nearly destroyed the entire complex.

### Other Notable Areas

- **Bothan Space:** An Allied Region in the Mid Rim of over 150 inhabited Bothan colonies, ruled by the Bothan Council and clans.
- **The Centrality:** A small, remote, semi-autonomous region at the far reaches of the Outer Rim. Known for asteroid-based casinos in the Oseon system.
- **Hapes Cluster:** Home to the isolationist Hapes Consortium, a matriarchal society dominated by royalty, etiquette, and political intrigue. Surrounded by the Transitory Mists.
- **Herglic Space:** An Allied Region of about forty systems near the Rimma Trade Route. The Empire seized control and now mercilessly rules the Herglics.
- **Mandalorian Space:** Home region of the Mandalorian warriors. Largely meant the Mandalore Sector by the time of the Clone Wars. Boba Fett is the best-known representative of this warrior culture.
- **Shadowports:** Operate without regard to government regulation. Found throughout the galaxy, mostly in the Outer Rim. Major shadowports include Nar Shaddaa, Kwenn Space Station, Tatooine, Terminus, Bespin, and Socorro.
- **Tion Cluster:** On the Perlemian Trade Route in the Outer Rim. Home to one of the most ancient Human societies. The Tion Hegemony within it supports a variety of illegal and semi-legal operations.
